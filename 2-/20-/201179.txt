Ich zitiere Clemens Obitz:
"Though I didn't always agree with the powers-that-were at Fox in 1989,
there was a definite sense that we were all working together to put the
best, most effective version of the film in theaters.
And I had, by contract, final cut.
But we had a picture on our hands which was much too long in our collective
opinion. And after the first test-market screenings, we also found out that
it wasn't playing so well. The scene that the Dallas preview audience
seemed to object to the most was the tidal wave sequence. It was the single
most-mentioned scene when they answered the "Scenes I liked the least"
blank on the questionnaire. Curiously, it was also the most-mentioned scene
in the "Scenes I liked the most" category.
Clearly we had a film that played very well to a broad audience for 90% of
its length, and then went badly south for half that audience on the
one-yard line. That bifurcated response probably represented the different
ways people respond to material in general; the wave sequence was
imaginative, almost surreal, and was the culmination of a thematic line in
the film which worked at an intellectual or conceptual level rather than an
amotional one.
[...]
I elected to remove the wave sequence and unweave from the film the subplot
which builds up to it - the newscasts which show the world inexorably
slipping toward the brink of a nuclear abyss. I also accelerated the
credits to the speed of a roll-up blind, thus pissing off about a thousand
people who worked on the film. But it saved us another minute or so. That,
along with the removal of some establishing scenes of business-as-usual on
the oil rig and development of the secondary characters shrank the film to
a puny 2 hours and 20 minutes."

So. Sehr lang, aber sehr informativ.
