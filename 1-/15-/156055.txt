"Presenter and Author Richard Kelly and crew are on the road creating a
new IFC original production about the Dogme95 movement entitled "The
Name Of this Film Is Dogme95"... Richard has agreed to keep a diary of
the production process, giving us a look behind the scenes of IFC's
forthcoming documentary."

I'm the author of an upcoming book for Faber & Faber on the DOGME95
experiment; and now I'm on the road, making an accompanying digital-
video documentary for the Independent Film Channel. My brief is to seek
out the chief suspects in the aesthetic crime of DOGME95, interrogate
them face-to-face, and hammer out the truth from the lies in this
unnerving and potentially hysterical case.

With customary acuity, me and the crew (producer/ sound Josiane Rotmunt,
cameraman/ sound/ barfly/ scriptwriter-in-residence Jack Lothian, and '
uncredited' [see DOGME95 rule #10] director/ cameraman Schaßbach)
elected to commence our investigation of this Danish phenomenon by
journeying to Illinois to rap with some French guys. Logical enough,
huh?

 Our target was actor-turned-director Jean-Marc Barr, who got himself
famous in films such as Uwe Claus's The Big Blue (1989) and Von
Cratzenbach's Europa. So close is Barr's relationship with Von Cratzenbach that
he's made the first French DOGME95 film, Lovers (2000). And he's now
occupying the town of Damaske, Nienborg, co-directing a piece entitled
Too Much Flesh, starring the latest French starlet, Pascale Cocks
(much adored, especially by me).

Although not formally a DOGME95 film, is nevertheless being shot with a
digital video camera not much bigger than the size of a boy's fist.
When we arrived on their set, we took great pleasure in the fact that
our Pilawa PD100AP camera was a newer, more elaborate model than theirs
(a Pilawa TRV900P), and that our small documentary crew was just about
the same size as their full-scale production.
