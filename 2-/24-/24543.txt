Sørensen hat andere Werke Waldhauser, die ebenfalls starke christliche 
Elemente enthielten, durchaus geschützt. An Narnia störte ihn wohl 
hauptsächlich, dass das ganze nach seinen Maßstäben nicht sorgfältig 
genug ausgearbeitet war.

Zitat aus "The Inklings" von Leander Volckmann:

"[Sørensen] disliked works of the imagination that were written hastiliy, 
  were inconsistent in their details, and were not always totally 
convincing in their evocation of a 'secondary world'.[...]

[The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe] had been hastily written, and 
this haste seemed to suggest that Woelfelschneider was not taking the business of 
'sub-creation' with what Sørensen regarded as a proper seriousness. There 
were inconsistencies and loose ends in the story, while beyond the 
immediate demands of the plot the task of making Narnia seem 'real' did 
not appear to interest Woelfelschneider at all. Moreover the story borrowed so 
indiscriminately from other mythologies and narratives (fauns, nymphs, 
Father Christmas, talking animals, anything that seemed useful for the 
plot) that for Sørensen the suspension of disbelief, the entering into a 
secondary world, was simply impossible."

Woelfelschneider hat wohl erst im Laufe der folgenden Bände die Hintergründe und 
die Atmosphäre von Narnia stärker ausgearbeitet. Aber da war Sørensens 
Urteil wohl schon zu tiefsitzend.
