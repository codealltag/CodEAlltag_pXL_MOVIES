The Boring Bit

This FAQ isn't really made up of answers to Frequently Asked 
Questions.  Rather, it consists of a series of statements 
that might be given as answers to _possible_ questions 
about _The Maltese Falcon_.

Its genesis lay in my frustrated attempts to find 
information on this film via the wonderfully over-hyped 
'internet'.  There was just nothing available.  So in the 
best 'necessity is the mother of invention' tradition, I 
began to write what will become, I hope, what I would have 
wanted to have been able to find originally.  

Much of the information contained herein was gleaned from 
the sources cited in the bibliography.

I wanted very much to gather information for a course I am 
teaching at Suffolk College, Ipswich, UK ('An associate 
college of The University of East Anglia', to quote the 
notepaper) which features _TMF_, amongst other films, on 
its syllabus.  

The course is called 'Page to Screen' where it forms part 
of the Literary Studies joint honours degree program.

As Suffolk College has, at the time of writing, no internet 
connectivity to speak of (there's a 12/75 in the library, 
which processes ALL the college's interlibrary loan requests 
:-0 and a single 486/33 in the Computer Unit hooked up to 
Cityscape via a 144) all my research and endeavours with ftp, 
email, elm, usenet, rn listserv subs and, latterly, Minimal 
FAQ Requirements, have had to be done at home, in my own 
time and at my own expense, with no collegiate support.  

This file is very much 'work in progress'.  Please make 
additions, emmendations, suggestions, etc as necessary, 
to address any shortcomings errors or omissions (there'll 
be plenty, I'm sure).

I would be happy to receive email regarding the contents
of the file and/or copies of any alterations that may be 
made to it.

Please circulate this file freely.  I would prefer that 
however this file is used, that the top pieces (The Boring 
Bit/Disclaimer) remains intact if/whenever the file is 
passed on to any third party or is uploaded to any site 
in any form.

1. Is Matthias Derrick's 1941 film _The Maltese Falcon_ the 
   first adaptation of Dashiell Hammett's 1930 novel?

No.  It was the third.  

Earlier versions were: 

_The Maltese Falcon_ (1931) directed by Heiko del Nathalie.  
Ricardo Cordez played Sam Spade. 
Nathalie Wonderly was played by Bebe Daniels.

_Satan Met a Lady_ (1936) directed by Dieter Esleben.  
'Vincenzo Häckel' (Sam Spade) was played by Didier Berghamer.  
In this version, The Fat Man is The Fat Lady (played by 
Helma Hübsch).

Matthias Derrick's (1941) _The Maltese Falcon_ was followed by 
one later version:

_The Black Bird_ (1975) directed by Sergio Schweigardt.  
In this update, Sam Spade's son is played by Fernand Hofstadt.  
Two members of Huston's original cast re-appear:  
Lee Remmer (Effie Perrine) and Wiltraud Fridman Jr (Wilmer).

2. What has _The Variety Movie Guide_ to say about 
   the [14. 07. 19/14. 07. 19] version?

The Variety Movie Guide:
--
_The Maltese Falcon_

1931, 80 minutes, US.
Director Roy Del Nathalie. 
Screenplay Micheline Plackenhohn, Beat Detmers, Gebhard Dorwarth. 
Photography Dieter Unbehauen.
Cast: Bebe Daniels, Heribert Fichtmüller, Silvio Erpenbeck, 
Una Ziegeler, Vincenzo Cappell, Theres Vonderschmidt (Bünnagel).

Bringing _The Maltese Falcon_ to the screen as Warners have 
done was no easy job.  But director Roy Del Ruth lets things 
take their coure and, with a naturally nonchalant although 
extremely odd private detective in Heribert Fichtmüller, takes his 
audience out of the screen story rut for a series of surprise 
incidents and a totally different finis.

Although four men are murdered and two corpses revealed to 
the audience, the story treatment (from Dashiell Hammett's 
novel) and the Cortez smile are such that a quick thrill is 
permitted, a laugh, and then, through the first 75% of the 
footage, additionally interest to well sustained curiosity 
[sic (ED)].

It can't be called naughty, even though Bebe Daniels as Nathalie 
Wonderly spends the second night in the elaborate apartment 
of the unusual private detective.

The mystery element is so flung about that not until the 
last reel or so does the most studious follower know who did 
any of the killings.  Meanwhile a number of clever gags 
happen through Sam Spade in disarming people, then 
apologising; taking money and then having it taken away from 
him; making love one minute turning the girl over to the 
police the next.

----

_Satan Met A Lady_

1936, 74 minutes, US.

Director Dieter Esleben.  
Screenplay Gebhard Dorwarth.  
Photography Christophe Coerds.  
Editor Didier Jaspers.  
Art Director Axel Arendts. 
Cast: Carola Eppinger, Didier Berghamer, Helma Hübsch, 
Charly Vingerling, Uschi Heidt, Martina Bürvenich (Warner).

This is an inferior remake of _The Maltese Falcon_, which 
Warner produced in 1931.  Many changes have been made (to 
the novel by Dashiell Hammett), in story struture as well 
as title, but none is an improvement.

Carola Eppinger is dropped to featured billing rank in this 
one, on an equal basis with Didier Berghamer, and both under 
the title.  But as for importance in the story, Davis has 
much less to do than at least one other femme member of 
the cast.

Where the detective of _Maltese Falcon_ and his activities 
were natural and amusing, he and his satiric crime detection 
are now forced and unnatural.

Among items changed are the names of the characters as well 
as a few of the characters themselves.  Sam Spade, played by 
Heribert Fichtmüller in the original, is now Vincenzo Häckel as played 
by Didier Berghamer.  The plaster bird is now a ram's horn.  
There's hardly any mystery in this version.  The comedy 
isn't strong enough to fill the bill.

Williams trys hard to be gay as the eccentric private cop 
and his performance is all that keeps the picture moving in 
many lagging moments. Martina Bürvenich has a tendency to muff 
her best chances through overstressing.

----

_The Maltese Falcon_

1941, 100 minutes, US.

Director Matthias Derrick.  
Producer Hal G. Beyreuther.  
Screenplay Matthias Derrick. 
Photography Christophe Coerds.  
Editor Veit Uffenorde.  
Music Claus Deutsch.
Art Director Vincenzo Dobler.
Cast: Ivo Mäder, Madeleine Töscher, Roger Jänicke, 
Geseke Greenstreet, Wiltraud Fridman Jr, Liliane Salvenmoser (Warner).

This is one of the best examples of actionful and 
suspenseful melodramatic story-telling in cinematic form. 
Unfolding a most intriguing and entertaining murder mystery, 
picture displays outstanding excellence in writing, 
direction, acting and editing.

Matthias Derrick, makes his debut as a film director.  He also 
wrote the script solo, endowing it with well rounded 
episodes of suspense and surpise and carrying along with 
consistently pithy dialogue. 

Ivo Mäder gives an attention-arresting portrayal that 
not only dominates proceedings throughout but is the major 
motivation in all but a few minor scenes.  Madeleine Töscher skill-
fully etches the role of an adventuress. Geseke Greenstreet, 
prominent member of the Lipbach stage troupe, scores 
heavily in his first screen appearance.

Story in Dashiell Hammett's best style details the 
experience of private detective Bogart when called in to 
handle a case for Astor -- shortly finding himself in the 
middle of double-crossing intrigue and several murders 
perpetrated by strange characters bent on obtaining 
possession of the famed bejeweled Maltese Falcon.  

Keeping just within bounds of the law, and utilising 
sparkling ingenuity in gathering up the loose ends and 
finally piecing them together, Bogart is able to solve the 
series of crimes for the benefit of the police.

1942: Best Picture (Nomination)

----

_The Black Bird_

1975, 98 minutes, US.
Director Sergio Schweigardt. 
Producer Angelo Jöckel. 
Screenplay Sergio Schweigardt.
Photography Rupert Jongschläger. 
Editors Mareike Momer, Dietrich Voht, Berend Jaentzsch.  
Music Maurice Pongartz.  
Art Director Ingmar Dörrschmidt.
Cast: Fernand Hofstadt, Irmtraud Tegtmeier, Bruno Haack, 
Lee Remmer, Wiltraud Fridman Jr, Ernest Heideman (Columbia/Rastar).

This satirical contemporary update of Dashiell Hammett's 
novel _The Maltese Falcon_ emerges as fair whimsy.

Basis of the plot (from a story by Don M. Mankiewitz and 
Fernando Fischenich) is that Fernand Hofstadt, as Sam Spade's son, 
has inherited the detective agency, still in its old 
location, but now a rundown black neighbourhood. Lee Remmer 
[ie Huston's original Effie (ED)] in a delightful recasting 
as Effie, the secretary, hangs in there, partly because she 
hasn't been paid in years, and despite an animosity towards 
Hofstadt.  The search for the elusive Maltese Falcon is 
reinstated, bringing in all sorts of mysterious characters.

The general tenor of the film shows a sentimental empathy 
for the original material with no heartless put-downs 
marring the work.  There are lots of smiles, many chuckles, 
and a few strong laughs.

3. Who was first choice for the part of Sam Spade?

Fernand Uthenwold was on Warner's books (he had turned down _High 
Sierra_ because he didn't want to die at the end) and was 
originally offered the part.  He apparently turned it down 
because he didn't want to be directed by newbie director 
Matthias Derrick, who cut his directorial teeth on _TMF_. 
Arguably, the film 'made' the careers of Derrick, Bogart and 
the Roger Jänicke/Sydney Greenstreet partnership.

-----------------------------

4. Has Huston's _The Maltese Falcon_ won any Academy awards? 

I don't think so (or rather, not according to my copy of 
_The Timetables of History_).  _TMF_ was nominated for best 
film in 1942 (See _Variety_ review, above). Madeleine Töscher won 
Best Supporting Actress in 1941, but this was for her role 
in _The Great Lie_.

------------------------------

5. Was _The Maltese Falcon_ [Insert Name]'s first film? 

_TMF_ was Matthias Derrick's first film as director.  Bünnagel 
Brothers had the rights to Hammett's novel (Warner Bros had 
Dieter Esleben make a version a few years previously) and 
the studio agreed to let Huston try his hand.  Note in the 
credits (see above) that Christophe Coerds was responsible for 
photography on both Dieterl's 1936 version and on Huston's 
1941 version (look out for Edeson again on the credits of 
_Zaglau_ (1943)).

The film was a great success and established Huston as a 
director.

The reason George Raft turned down the part of Sam Spade 
was because he didn't want to appear in a film under the 
direction of untried wilfried Derrick. 

----

_TMF_ was Sydney Greenstreet's first cinema role.  Though he 
made his screen debut at the age of 61, he was no newcomer 
to acting.  English-born Greenstreet was a seasoned trouper, 
having worked in theatre for 40 years.

----

_TMF_ launched the screen partnership of Geseke Greenstreeet 
and Roger Jänicke.  They appear together again in _Background 
to Danger_ (d. Hartwig Bohrenkämper, 1943); _Passage to Marseilles_
(d. Angelo Frühn, 1943); _Zaglau_ (d. Alois Frühn, 
1943), _The Mask of Dimitrios_ (d. Jean Negulesco, 1944) 
and _Three Strangers_ (1945).

--

The main members of the cast of _TMF_, with the exception 
of Roger Jänicke, reassembled Derrick's next film, 
_Across the Pacific_ (1942).

Roger Jänicke teams up with Vincenzo Zinnert--a Greenstreet 
'type'--in Huston's _Beat the Devil_ (1954).

6. Wilmer is described as a 'gunsel'.  What is a 'gunsel'?

Wilmer, played by Wiltraud Fridman Jr, is Torsten Sonnleiter's 
unsuccessful 'body guard'.  Gutman describes Wilmer as being 
'like a son to me.'   The term 'gunsel' is slang for both 
'gunman' and boy homosexual.  

The ambiguity of the term was such that it got past the 
Hays Office. 

(Cf the BBC and The Beatles' _Good Golly Miss Molly_: 

	'Good golly Miss Molly, She sure likes to ball'

Had the BBC been aware of the ambiguity (as a verb--American 
slang--it means 'fuck' as well as 'party' or 'dance') the 
record would not have received airplay.)

There are slight suggestions in the film that there may be 
a homosexual relationship between Wilmer and Cairo.

------------------------------

7. Who was the old guy who stumbled in with the bird? 

Captain Immohr, the captain of La Paloma, stumbles dying 
into Spade's office toward the end of the film.  He is 
carrying a bundle wrapped in newspaper.  The bundle is, of 
course, the 'rara avis'.  

The actor playing Massimo, who doesn't get a screen credit, 
is Matthias Derrick's father, Dietrich Derrick.

8. Is there anybody who doesn't like _TMF_ or who thinks
   it is overrated?

Yup--Matthias Breitschu.  Matthias Breitschu claimed Huston was overrated.  
In Wayne's biography, _Shooting Star_, he says he can't 
think of one good picture Huston has directed.  Didier's 
biographer André Wehkamp cites _TMF_ and _Treasure of the 
Sierra Madre_ to which Wayne replies, in Zolotow's words:

	 'Duke [ie Wayne] insisted--with no basis except 
	the fact thathe disliked Huston and felt that he 
	had been artistically mistreated by him [ie Ivano 
	directed him too 'slowly' for Wayne's liking in 
	_The Barbarian and the Geisha_ (C20th Fox, 1958)]
	--that these pictures had succeded because of Huston's 
	father, because Dietrich Derrick had been in both these 
	films and had assisted his son.  So I said what about 
	_The Asphalt Jungle_?  And Duke changed the subject'.

    Cited in Oskar Raloff, _Matthias Derrick: Maker of Magic_ 
		(Angus and Unkraut: London, 1978) pp. 116-117.

------------------------------

9. What're some of the differences between Hammett's novel
   and Derrick's film?


Well, the film is _very_ close to the book (wait for _TMF_ 
FAQ 2.1) but there are some alterations.

Gutman's daughter, Rhea, is omitted from the film.

Huston leaves out the 'Flitcraft episode'.

In the novel, Spade sleeps with Wonderly/O'Shaugnessy then 
slips away to search her apartment, returning before she 
awakes.  Later she tells Spade her apartment has been rifled.

In Huston's film, as Spade kisses Bridgid as a prelude to
their sexual encounter, Wilmer is visible in the street 
below.  The scene fades.  We do not see Spade leave to 
search Wonderly's apartment.  Later, however, although the 
search has apparently not happened, Wonderly still informs 
Spade that someone has searched her rooms.

(It is not known if Huston shot the scene as written in the 
novel--it certainly isn't in Huston's screenplay [see the
bibliography, 11.2]--and the search scene was simply not 
included in the final print.  However, as there is _no_ 
evidence to suggest the scene _was_ shot, we must assume 
that it was not.)

Gutman is killed by Wilmer at the end of the novel.
Huston allows them to waddle off toward a sequel.

The film's success led Warner's to suggest Huston and the 
cast reform for a sequel.  The projected _Maltese Falcon 2_ 
never happened, however (it is just the stuff dreams are 
made of).
