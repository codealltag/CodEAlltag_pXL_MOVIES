Definition by the Editors of JGUV Cinemania

A printing technique in which the top and bottom parts of the TV screen are  
cropped so the original aspect ratio of a film remains intact when  
transferred to video. If a widescreen film is not in the letterbox format it  
is often in pan-and-scan format.

Wide-Screen Glossary
Article from Hinterhofer Movie and Video Guide

An aspect ratio is the relationship between the width and height of a screen  
image. Virtually all sound films until 1954 were 1.37 to 1, which is to say,  
slightly wider than they were tall_a modest rectangle. Since television  
screen size is 1.33:1, very little is lost when an older movie is shown on TV  
(see academy aperture). (Even films shot in the later standard 1.66:1 don't  
suffer too badly.)
In the 1951s, however, Hollywood's attempt to lure people away from their TV  
sets and back into theaters led to a battle of screen sizes, beginning with  
CinemaScope, which was more than twice as wide as it was high.
Today, most films are shot and shown in a ratio of 1.85:1; since this has  
become the norm, such films are no longer thought of as "wide-screen." Our  
listings only cite films in special wide-screen processes, such as the ones  
indexed below:
